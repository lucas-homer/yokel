DocketClock · A briefing for conservation partners · July 2026

Public participation runs on deadlines. DocketClock makes them trustworthy.

Every year, federal agencies make thousands of decisions about water, land, wildlife, and health — and nearly every one opens a short window when anyone can weigh in. DocketClock is a working system that watches those windows around the clock, catches them when they move, and is honest about what it doesn’t know.

01Why this exists

Comment windows are where policy is still open — and they are surprisingly hard to trust

When a federal agency proposes a rule — protecting a wetland, permitting a pipeline, revising water-quality standards — it is legally required to ask the public first. A comment period isn’t a petition or a vote: a single well-evidenced comment from someone who knows the ground truth can change the final rule, and comments in the record are what later court challenges stand on. But only if the comment arrives before the window closes.

Here’s the catch. The deadline itself lives in two separate government systems:

  • The Federal Register — the government’s official daily journal. The legal record of what was proposed and when comments are due.
  • Regulations.gov — the website where you actually read the docket and file your comment.

They are maintained separately, and they don’t always agree. On top of that:

  • Extensions are published as separate documents that never update the original notice.
  • Corrections sometimes move a deadline earlier, not later.
  • One notice can change several proceedings at once — each with a different new date.
  • Headlines mislead: a “land withdrawal extension” from the Bureau of Land Management is about land, not about extending your comment period.

For a conservation organization, a missed window means silence in the legal record. And a wrong date is worse than no date at all — it manufactures false confidence.

02What DocketClock does

Four verbs: watch, remember, reconcile, admit

1Watch

It checks both government systems continuously — every fifteen minutes — across every open federal comment window, over a thousand at any given time.

2Remember

Every observation goes into a permanent logbook that can never be edited or erased. Any deadline it publishes can be traced back to exactly what the government said, word for word, and when.

3Reconcile

It merges what both systems claim into a single answer, following the whole chain of extensions, corrections, and reopenings — and labels that answer with how much it should be trusted.

4Admit

When the sources disagree, it doesn’t guess. It says so, shows both dates side by side, links the official record — and after every deadline passes, it goes back and grades its own answer.

03The honesty principle

Every deadline carries a confidence label. None of them bluff.

Most trackers hand you a date and imply certainty they don’t have. DocketClock treats the confidence label as part of the product:

LabelWhat it meansWhat you can do with it
High Both systems agree, the information is fresh, and no extension or correction is unresolved. Plan around it.
Medium Only one source has spoken, or there’s a minor wrinkle (say, the agency accepts late comments). Usable, with the wrinkle spelled out.
Low Signals are thin — for example, a docket marked “open for comment” with no date given. Worth watching, not worth scheduling around.
Conflicting The two systems genuinely disagree, or an extension is in flight and unresolved. You get the disagreement itself: both dates, side by side, with the official source linked.
Stale It hasn’t been possible to re-confirm recently enough, relative to how soon the window closes. Verify before relying on it — and it tells you that.
Unknown No deadline has been published anywhere. An honest “we don’t know” instead of a guess.

Silence is never the failure mode. When confidence drops, DocketClock withholds the confident “closing soon” alert — but simultaneously sends a “needs verification” alert with the conflicting evidence attached. You are never left unaware because the system was unsure.

05A story about discipline

We already designed a conservation app — and killed it with data

The first idea built on DocketClock was Watershed Watch: a deadline-and-action radar scoped to a single watershed, designed for volunteer conservation coalitions. The pilot geography was the Choptank River basin on the Delmarva Peninsula.

Before building it, we asked the question that kills most civic tools too late: is there enough real signal here to matter? We measured four quarters of federal activity mentioning the basin, its tributaries, and its counties — then hand-checked every candidate:

16
candidate matches over four quarters — the optimistic upper bound.
11
were national rules that merely mention “Chesapeake Bay” — nothing to do with the Choptank.
3
matched “Talbot County”… the one in Georgia.
2
were the Bay Bridge crossing study — high-profile, out of basin, and already known to everyone.
0
confirmed novel in-basin windows. Zero, across a full year.

The bar for a viable product was two to three genuinely new, in-basin comment windows per quarter. The measured answer was zero per year. So Watershed Watch was shelved — before it consumed months of anyone’s life or a partner’s trust.

The lesson isn’t that conservation tools don’t work. It’s that a single small watershed doesn’t generate enough federal decisions on its own. The idea fails or succeeds on where you point it — a region, a topic, a species, a program. And choosing where the signal lives is a policy question, not a software question. That’s exactly where a domain expert changes the outcome.

06The primitive

DocketClock is a foundation. New apps rent it — they don’t rebuild it.

Everything hard about the deadline problem is solved once, underneath. Any new application built on DocketClock inherits, on day one:

  • The full live stream of every open federal comment window, hearing notice, and environmental-review clock — with deadlines that have been cross-checked, not copied.
  • A confidence label and a complete paper trail on every date, back to the government’s own words.
  • Stable identifiers, so records can be joined with maps, watershed boundaries, species ranges, or membership lists.
  • A tagging layer: each app adds its own lens — watershed codes, species, land units — without touching the core engine.
  • Honest alerting: conflicts fire loudly, and uncertainty is never silent.

Which means building a new app is mostly three decisions: who it’s for, what lens it applies (a geography, a topic, an agency), and what action it supports once a window is found.

07What could be built on it

Ideas for conservation — some are Watershed Watch with the lens fixed

Watch · Geography

Regional Watershed Radar

Watershed Watch, widened to where the signal density actually is: an entire basin like the Chesapeake or Delaware, or a multi-basin coalition footprint. Every window tagged to the watersheds it touches, delivered as plain-language briefs.

Watch · Process

Environmental Review Clock

Environmental impact statements run on their own rhythm — a 45-day review window for drafts, 30 for finals, and no one system that tracks them well. A radar for EIS review windows by region or sector.

Watch · Species

Species & Habitat Docket Desk

Endangered-species listings, critical-habitat designations, take permits — every open window touching a species list an organization actually works on, instead of scanning the whole Federal Register.

Watch · Land

Public Lands Watch

Forest plan revisions, BLM land-use amendments, withdrawals, and leasing decisions — comment windows organized by the forests and districts a group cares about.

Watch · Water

Water Rule Watch

The recurring heavyweights: “waters of the United States” definitions, water-quality standards, discharge and dredge-and-fill permitting. Rules where the same fight returns every few years and the window is everything.

Act · Quality

Comment Quality Room

Agencies discount thousands of identical form letters. A tool that helps a coalition turn many member voices into a few distinct, evidence-backed comments that actually enter the record with weight.

Act · Accountability

Outcome Tracker

The neglected other half: after the window closes, did the final rule respond? What changed? Receipts an organization can show members and funders — “here’s what our participation did.”

Act · Routing

Concern Router

Someone raises an alarm about a place or an issue. Which live proceeding is the actual lever — and when does it close? A bridge from ambient concern to the venue where input legally counts.

The first five are lenses on the watching problem; the last three use the same foundation to support the acting — which is where conservation organizations live.

08Where you come in

The software exists. The open questions are policy questions.

What killed Watershed Watch wasn’t engineering — it was pointing the instrument at too small a target. Every question that decides what gets built next is one a domain expert answers better than a developer:

  1. Where is the density?Which geographies, topics, or programs generate enough real comment windows to be worth watching? Any candidate can be measured in days — the same way the Choptank was.
  2. What counts as actionable?Of the windows a lens would surface, which would an organization genuinely act on — and which are noise it already knows about?
  3. What’s the workflow?Who inside a conservation org would use this — and what form helps them: a weekly brief, a phone alert, a calendar feed, a spreadsheet?
  4. Who else should see this?Which organizations or people in the conservation world feel this pain sharply enough to shape — or pilot — a first version?

A concrete first step: pick two or three candidate lenses — a region, a rule topic, a species list. We run the same value-density measurement that was run for the Choptank. The numbers, not enthusiasm, decide what gets built.

Honest limits. DocketClock covers federal proceedings today; state and local sources are deliberately out of scope until a specific need justifies them. It is an aid to participation, not legal advice — every record links directly to the official government publication, which is always the authority.
