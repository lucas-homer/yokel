DocketClock · a briefing for conservation partners

Never miss a public-comment window again.

Public participation in federal environmental decisions runs on deadlines. DocketClock is a system that watches those deadlines continuously, catches them when they move, and tells you — with a paper trail — exactly how long you have left to be heard.

Prepared July 2026 · all statistics measured against the live government APIs, June 2026

The problem

The comment window is where the public's power lives — and it's easy to lose.

When an agency proposes a rule, permits a project, or publishes an environmental impact statement, the law gives the public a window — often 30 to 60 days — to comment on the record.

  • Comments filed in the window shape the final decision — agencies must respond to substantive ones.
  • Missing the window can mean losing standing later — courts ask whether you raised the issue when you could.
  • The deadlines are scattered and unstable — published in two different government systems, extended, reopened, corrected, and withdrawn, often with little fanfare.

For a volunteer coalition or a small staffed org, just knowing what's open and when it truly closes is a part-time job nobody has.

  • Every window gets a confidence grade. When the two systems agree, you get a verified deadline. When they disagree, DocketClock says "these conflict — here are both dates and both official notices" instead of guessing.
  • Deadline changes are first-class events. Extension and reopening notices are linked back to the original window, so "you have 30 more days" reaches you as news, not luck.
  • Everything is traceable. Every claim links to the official government notice, and every change is kept in a permanent audit log — nothing is ever silently overwritten.
Why trust it

It grades its own homework.

A deadline tool is only useful if it's right. DocketClock is built to prove that, not assert it:

It checks itself after the fact

After every window closes, the system goes back and verifies whether the deadline it published was correct — building a running, public accuracy record instead of a marketing claim.

It's checked from the outside too

An independent archive of Regulations.gov data is used to cross-examine the system's records. Every disagreement gets investigated and classified.

Uncertainty is shown, never hidden

A conflicting record is flagged as conflicting, with both sources side by side. The rule inside the system: suppression is never silence.

Run like infrastructure

Continuous monitoring with alerts that page a phone, tested backups, and monthly restore drills that run automatically. Boring on purpose.

Honest limits

What it does not do (yet).

  • Federal only, today. State agency permit comment periods (state DEQ/DEP water permits, stormwater, wetlands) and local planning boards are not covered — and honestly, that's where many watershed fights actually live. Extending coverage is possible, but it's real work we'd scope with a partner.
  • It can't file your comment for you. Since 2025 the government blocks automated comment submission by outside tools (we verified this directly). The honest workflow is: DocketClock drafts and reminds, a human files, one click away.
  • It's an alert system, not legal advice. Every record carries a link to the official notice. Verify there before you rely on a deadline for anything with legal consequence.

We lead with the limits deliberately: the coverage promise you can rely on — "you will not miss a federal comment window on the dockets you watch" — is bounded and testable.

The bigger idea

Under the hood is a reusable primitive: a verified registry of participation windows.

DocketClock's real product isn't a website — it's a continuously verified answer to the question "where can the public act right now, and until when?" Anything that needs that answer can be built on top: a watershed radar, a regional digest, a coalition calendar, a policy tracker.

What this could become for conservation

Four ideas we'd love your reaction to.

Regional deadline radar

Every open federal comment window and EIS touching your region — with reminders at 14 days, 7 days, 48 hours. The honest lesson from our own testing: a single small watershed had essentially zero novel federal windows per quarter. The signal shows up at the scale of a region, a coalition, or a topic — which is why your network matters more than any one basin.

NEPA / EIS tracker

The Federal Register publishes ~50 environmental-impact-statement notices a month, each starting a 45- or 30-day statutory clock. A tracker that reconciles those clocks with later extension notices — nationally or by state — is a natural first build on the registry.

Coalition digest

A weekly "what opened, what moved, what closes soon" email for a coalition's chosen geographies and topics — with the audit trail one click away so organizers can forward it with confidence.

Accountability lens

The change history is data: which agencies extend most, which dockets get reopened, where deadlines quietly shrink. A watchdog view for journalists and policy shops — nobody has this today because nobody keeps the history.
