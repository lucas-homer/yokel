Rank #1 · Environment / Land use

Watershed Watch — a basin-scoped environmental deadline-and-action radar for volunteer conservation groups

Never miss the federal or state environmental decision affecting your creek — and turn your members into the radar for everything local.

16.7
avg / 25

The problem

Small volunteer-run watershed alliances, friends-of-the-creek groups, and land trusts are chronically late to the fights that matter most: a draft EIS on an upstream project, a stormwater/wetlands permit comment window, an EPA or state-agency rulemaking, a floodplain rezoning. The signals are scattered across Regulations.gov dockets, the Federal Register, the EPA EIS database, state legislation, and the fragmented local long tail. These groups have passion but no policy staff, so they miss short windows, file weak duplicate comments officials discount, and burn out because they cannot see whether any of it mattered. Existing tools are either lobbyist B2B (Quorum/Plural), Congress-only action (Resistbot/5 Calls), or government-side engagement portals (Granicus) — none deliver discovery + deadline + quality action + honest receipts to a non-expert volunteer scoped to a watershed.

Who it's for

Primary: volunteer leaders and 1-3 person staff of geographically-scoped conservation groups — watershed alliances, Waterkeeper affiliates, land trusts, friends-of-the-creek/-bay/-forest groups — who own a defined basin and have no policy capacity. Secondary: statewide environmental coalitions and regional water-board NGOs who buy org seats and act as the funded channel partner; and local newsrooms/other nonprofits who license the federal-deadline confidence feed.

The solution

Watershed Watch is a grant- and coalition-funded deadline-and-action service scoped to a single watershed. The user picks their basin from the USGS Watershed Boundary Dataset (a free national HUC primitive — no bespoke boundary drawing needed) and the system continuously catches federal and state environmental decisions whose geography is carried in standardized notice text and metadata: EPA EIS filings (searchable by state, with a fixed 45-day-draft / 30-day-final comment clock), Regulations.gov dockets and Federal Register notices, and state environmental legislation via Open States. Each hit becomes a first-class, alertable deadline object — comment-period close dates rendered as a calendar with lead-time reminders, which exists nowhere today. For each open window the user gets a plain-language brief and a structured-evidence comment composer that produces substantive, non-duplicate testimony officials actually weigh. The monitor->act loop closes with honest procedural receipts: filed-on-time confirmations, comment counts, and draft-to-final text diffs — never causal "you changed this" claims. The local long tail it cannot reliably scrape is handled by the members themselves: a NoticeNail-style flow lets volunteers photograph a posted site notice, geocode the parcel once, and surface it to the group — explicitly labeled "community signal, not comprehensive monitoring." Pol.is consensus is held in reserve only for genuinely contested multi-faction fights.

How it works

  • Onboard: the group picks its watershed from the USGS WBD (HUC-8/12) via The National Map REST service; the system resolves the intersecting states and counties so it knows which jurisdictions to watch.
  • Ingest Tier 1 (automated): poll Regulations.gov v4 + Federal Register API + the EPA EIS database + Open States v3 for environmental dockets/bills; RuleBox-style LLM classification rules extract geographic references and watershed-relevance, scoped to the group's HUC, states, and named water bodies.
  • Normalize + dedupe into the Open Civic Data model, attach the comment-period close date as a first-class deadline object, and write to a versioned archive (MAPLE pattern) with a Typesense index.
  • Alert + brief: the follow->event->feed->digest engine pushes a plain-language brief and a deadline-with-lead-time reminder to the group; coverage is labeled per area (Tier 1 reliable / Tier 3 best-effort / Tier 2 community-reported).
  • Act: the structured-evidence comment composer guides volunteers to a substantive, non-duplicate comment, captures address->district for real-person assurance, and files to Regulations.gov / agency portal, recording a filed-on-time receipt.
  • See impact honestly: the group's dashboard shows procedural receipts — comments filed, total docket comment count, and a draft-to-final text diff when the agency publishes — with no manufactured causality. Members add local long-tail signals via the site-notice capture flow.

Gap it fills

Leads with Gap 1 (comment-period deadlines as a first-class alertable object) on the highest-stakes, hardest-to-track, genuinely geo-bearing stream for these users — federal/state environmental dockets — which is net-new whitespace nobody renders as an alertable deadline today. It also hits Gap 3 (cross-source normalization into one watershed feed), Gap 4 (plain-language + guided action), and Gap 5 (the full monitor->act->see-impact loop in one consumer product with a built-in sustainability answer). It touches Gap 2 (land use) honestly via the community-reported sensor layer rather than pretending to scrape it. Not a duplicate: it is geography-and-mission-scoped for amateurs, unlike lobbyist B2B incumbents; it is constituent-side, unlike Granicus/PublicInput; and it integrates the environmental docket + deadline + organize + impact loop that City Tracker AI/Symbium slivers lack.

MVP scope

Smallest credible v1: pick a basin from the USGS WBD; automated Tier 1 monitoring of EPA EIS filings + Regulations.gov + Federal Register + Open States, scoped by HUC/state, with each open comment window rendered as a deadline object with email/SMS lead-time reminders; a plain-language brief and a structured-evidence comment composer that files to Regulations.gov with a filed-on-time receipt; and a simple dashboard showing comment counts + draft-to-final diffs. Defer NoticeNail capture, Pol.is, and all Legistar/Accela ingestion to fast-follows behind clear coverage labels. First geography/domain: ONE basin with ONE committed coalition partner — strongest candidate is the Chesapeake Bay watershed via a Waterkeeper affiliate or a Chesapeake Bay Foundation chapter (dense federal/state EIS and permit activity, an organized funded coalition, multi-state HUC that exercises the cross-jurisdiction logic), or as a smaller alternative, San Francisco Bay via Baykeeper.

Why now

Housing and infrastructure pressure is pushing development onto floodplains and wetlands, raising the volume and stakes of environmental reviews exactly when 2023 NEPA amendments imposed shorter, harder deadlines that make a missed window unrecoverable. Cheap LLM classification (RuleBox-style rules on commodity infra) finally makes firehose-scale geo-scoping of docket text affordable to a foundation-tier budget, and the federal/state environmental corpus is now fully mirrored and query-ready (Mirrulations + spicy-regs). The geo and deadline primitives that used to be the blocker — USGS WBD boundaries and the EPA EIS state/clock metadata — are free, standardized, and API-accessible today.

Business model

Three legs, channel-led and built into the idea rather than bolted on: (1) Foundation and environmental-justice grants fund the free Tier 1 monitoring for volunteer groups — the public good. (2) Paid org seats sold to staffed land trusts, Waterkeeper affiliates, and statewide coalitions who get multi-chapter admin, custom basins, and member-management — the channel that also drives distribution to their volunteer chapters. (3) A sideways, horizontal "DocketClock" confidence API/feed — the normalized federal/state environmental comment-deadline stream — licensed to other nonprofits and newsrooms; this is the revenue line with the broadest TAM and the least vertical-specific cost. The contradictory original line (selling the activists' dashboard to the opposing water board) is removed.

Defensibility

The moat is not the data (all sources are open) but the curated, tuned, watershed-scoped relevance layer plus the trusted distribution channel. Specifically: (a) a continuously human-tuned RuleBox classification corpus that correctly maps docket text to basins and avoids documented traps like BLM "extension/correction" false positives — this accuracy compounds and is expensive for a generalist to replicate; (b) coalition relationships and member-reported local signal that no incumbent has constituent-side; (c) the honest-receipts brand that activists trust. Adjacency risk is real: Granicus owns Legistar + EngagementHQ and could add a "watch this docket" alert, but Granicus is structurally a government-buyer vendor and will not credibly serve groups organizing against its own customers; FiscalNote/Plural is priced and built for lobbyists; Resistbot is Congress-only with no discovery. The thin spot is honest: a generic "DocketClock" alert is the most copyable piece, so defensibility leans on channel + tuned relevance + trust, not on the alert itself.

Key risks & mitigations

Risk: The honest wedge feels underwhelming versus the mission. The most emotionally charged fights (the local floodplain rezoning) are precisely the ones Tier 1 cannot reliably surface, so groups may judge the tool by what it misses, not what it catches.
Mitigation: Lead messaging on 'never miss a federal/state window again' (a real, demonstrable win) and validate with a design-partner before building that catching upstream EIS/permit windows is itself high-value; treat community-reported local signal as a clearly-labeled bonus, never a promise.
Risk: Geo-classification accuracy is a permanent tuning burden, not a solved step; the BLM extension/correction trap and watershed-relevance false positives/negatives erode trust fast.
Mitigation: Ship per-area confidence labels and a human-in-the-loop review queue for the coalition partner's staff; use spicy-regs/DuckDB over the Mirrulations corpus to build and regression-test classification rules against labeled history before going live in a basin.
Risk: The product is arguably DocketClock + a Waterkeeper skin; the vertical wrapper may not earn distribution or funding the bare registry can't.
Mitigation: Make the vertical earn its keep through the funded coalition channel and EJ-grant fit that a horizontal registry cannot access; if the vertical fails to add distribution, fall back to monetizing DocketClock horizontally — the architecture supports both.
Risk: Funding requires landing ONE committed coalition partner before ingestion has value, and environmental-funder appetite for civic infra (vs. direct organizing) is cyclical.

Rank #2 · Rulemaking / civic retention (the see-impact loop)

Closed the Loop — the post-action outcome receipt that ends the not-knowing

You commented. We watch the docket and text you what actually happened — in plain English, with the source, and your next move.

16.7
avg / 25

The problem

Busy non-experts who submit a comment or testimony hit a wall of silence afterward and conclude their effort was pointless, so they never come back. Agencies do publish response-to-comments and final rules, but no consumer product connects a specific person's submission to that downstream outcome, leaving Resistbot/5 Calls users at "sent" and Regulations.gov users with no follow-up at all. The result is a chronic abandonment problem that quietly kills every other product in this space.

Who it's for

Primary: the lapsed-but-caring civic actor who commented once on a rule or local issue, never heard back, and went quiet — reachable because a host action product captured their submission. Secondary: the campaign organizer or coalition staffer who must show their group "the work moved something" to keep volunteers mobilized, and the funded payer (advocacy org, agency, or newsroom) who wants a credible accountability signal.

The solution

Closed the Loop is not an impact-attribution company — the red team correctly kills that. It is a conservative, source-quoting outcome-and-next-step watchdog that lives INSIDE an action flow that owns the submission event. At submission, it captures the user's own comment text plus the Regulations.gov trackingNbr, docketId, and document objectId, so it never has to re-find the comment in the public record (sidestepping the GAO-21-103181 re-find failure where 5-30% of commenters denied submitting and most comments lacked emails). It then watches that specific docket/document; when a decision lands it delivers one plain-language message that (1) reports the outcome with a verbatim govinfo/Federal Register link, (2) at THEME level quotes the agency's own response-to-comments on the user's concern ("the agency responded to this issue, which you and many others raised") — never an individual causal claim — and (3) routes to the next real lever. The monitor half is a reconcile-and-flag deadline/outcome engine over Federal Register + Regulations.gov; the act half is the next-action router (appeal window, related hearing, reintroduction, the decision-maker to lobby). The LLM is a retriever/triager, not a judge: low-confidence or high-stakes matches degrade to "we found the decision, here is the document" rather than a generated claim.

How it works

  • At submission inside the host action product, store the user's comment text, trackingNbr, docketId, and document objectId, plus a follow record keyed to OCD-ID and Regulations.gov identifiers — provenance captured, never reconstructed.
  • A reconcile-and-flag watcher polls Federal Register API + Regulations.gov v4 (and Mirrulations/spicy-regs for history) on the docket: it chains extension/reopening/correction/withdrawal documents, resolves FR-vs-Regulations.gov date conflicts, and emits a status (pending / final rule / withdrawn / rejected / no-change-yet) with a confidence flag, never silently picking one source.
  • When a final rule or terminal status posts, a retrieval step pulls the agency's response-to-comments section and finds the THEME matching the user's stored comment text via embeddings over the spicy-regs/Mirrulations corpus — returning the agency's verbatim language, not a generated verdict.
  • A guardrail layer gates output: high-confidence theme match -> quote the agency + diff; low confidence or high-stakes -> degrade to status-only 'we found the decision, here is the document.' No 'your point mattered' sentence is ever auto-sent.
  • Deliver one plain-language receipt over the user's messaging channel (SMS/WhatsApp/email) with the verbatim source link, the theme-level quote (or status-only fallback), and exactly one named next action with its real deadline.
  • Log every receipt to a versioned public archive (MAPLE pattern) so claims are auditable, and feed re-engagement (did they click, did they take the next action) back as the retention metric that proves or kills the thesis.

Gap it fills

Primarily Gap 5 (the see-impact half of the loop that no consumer product owns) and Gap 4 (plain-language legibility). It is not a duplicate: Resistbot/5 Calls stop at 'sent'; Regulations.gov has zero follow-up; Quorum/FiscalNote analytics are B2B lobbyist-facing and never tell an individual constituent what happened to their specific submission; FRTracker (the closest prior art per docketclock-research/prior-art.md) indexes FR + Regulations.gov comments and ships open-comment alerts but has NO post-action, per-user outcome receipt and explicitly no deadline-reconciliation algorithm. The novelty is the glue: provenance captured at submission + conservative theme-level outcome retrieval + next-action routing, delivered as one personal message.

MVP scope

Start with the Wizard-of-Oz gate BEFORE any build: hand-write outcome receipts for ~200 followers of ONE high-traffic, near-terminal federal docket (an EPA or HUD rule with a known imminent final action, e.g. an item in the EPA-HQ-OW or HUD AFFH lineage that already has response-to-comments posted) and measure re-engagement against a held-out control. First geography/domain: federal rulemaking only, single agency cluster (environment or housing) where final rules are actually landing now — not a slow pipeline. Only if re-engagement moves do you build v1: provenance capture at submission for ONE host action flow, the reconcile-and-flag watcher (FR + Regulations.gov, extension/reopening chaining, conflict flagging), the retrieval+guardrail receipt generator with the status-only fallback as default, and SMS/email delivery. Local/Legistar/land-use outcomes are explicitly OUT of scope until separately validated.

Why now

The proposed-vs-final diff and semantic match of a user's comment to the agency's response-to-comments only became cheap when Mirrulations mirrored the full Regulations.gov corpus to AWS Open Data and spicy-regs wrapped it in Parquet/DuckDB + an MCP server — turning a former analyst task into a tractable retrieval query. Messaging-native delivery (SMS/WhatsApp) and the RuleBox 'LLM generates cheap classifiers' pattern make firehose-scale triage affordable, so the only thing standing between 'sent' and 'here is what happened' is now product discipline, not data availability.

Business model

Funded payer is never the budget-light individual. Three credible buyers, all aligned with the repo's market-signals evidence: (1) advocacy orgs / coalitions running campaigns who pay to retain mobilized volunteers (embedded member-alert workflow, the 'show your group it worked' job — the associations/coalitions segment flagged as high-urgency); (2) newsrooms wanting an accountability signal feed; (3) agencies/B2G via a SocietyWorks-style commercial arm that funds the free consumer surface. Packaging follows the market-signals bands: a Pro embedded feed at $200-$1,000/month for orgs and a team/firm tier at $10k-$30k/year for unlimited members + audit logs + provenance trails. The capability is sold as retention infrastructure for action products, not as a standalone consumer subscription — which the portfolio repeatedly shows fails on funding.

Defensibility

The moat is being glued to the submission event and the user relationship, not the data — every source it uses is free/public, so a pure data play has no moat (FRTracker, Apify at $0.35/1k docs, and Regulations.gov itself prove the raw layer is commodity). The problem

The development that reshapes a neighborhood is rarely a dramatic rezoning — it's the steady drip of demolitions, ADUs, by-right additions, curb cuts, grading permits, and alcohol/cannabis licenses that each fly under the radar but collectively change a block. Residents have no aggregated, legible view, and for the minority of items where input still has a legal window (design review, variances, conditional-use permits, license objections), that window is short, buried in a hearing agenda, and never surfaced as a deadline. The raw data exists in Socrata/Accela/ArcGIS as cryptic permit codes residents can't parse, and the hearing schedule lives separately in Legistar/Granicus agendas.

Who it's for

Primary: neighborhood-association leaders and engaged residents who want a low-effort "what's changing around here" feed plus a clear signal on the rare items where their input still has a legal window. Secondary: local small-business owners (watching nearby competitor licenses, grading, and street work) and, on the revenue side, the incumbents who want the normalized corpus (real estate, insurance, BuildZoom-type lead tools) and city planning departments wanting better resident noticing.

The solution

Permit Pulse is the join product between the local permit/license firehose and the Planning/Zoning/Design-Review agenda calendar. It ingests each city's permit feed through interchangeable adapters (Socrata, Accela, ArcGIS — BLDS is treated as one possible source, never the thesis), and normalizes cryptic rows into resident-meaningful categories using RuleBox-style LLM-generated classification rules that then run cheaply at firehose scale. Separately it pulls structured meeting events and agenda items from the Legistar/Granicus OData API (the events endpoint gives real future hearing dates; an LLM parses the free-text/PDF body to extract the case, address, and project type), then joins the two by address and parcel. The product surfaces two honestly-labeled tiers: an AWARENESS feed of by-right activity ranked by impact x proximity (sold purely as early warning and neighbor-organizing, with no false promise of a lever) and a LEVER feed of discretionary items carrying a real hearing date, an inferred comment/appeal window, and a routed action path that can escalate into a "Block Whip" neighbor campaign. Each weekly digest leads with the honest headline — "Nothing needs you this week" or "2 items have an open window" — making candor the trust mechanism, backed by a published, transparent coverage map so misses read as known boundaries rather than failures.

How it works

  • Resident enters an address; MapIt-style geocoding resolves it to parcel, council district, and the relevant planning/zoning bodies, and sets a proximity radius (default: same block + adjacent parcels, expandable).
  • Permit adapters poll the city's Socrata/Accela/ArcGIS feed; RuleBox-generated rules classify each raw row into a resident category (teardown, ADU, big addition, curb cut, license) and an impact score; new nearby items land in the AWARENESS feed.
  • A parallel Legistar/Granicus job pulls upcoming Planning Commission / Zoning Board / Design Review events with real EventDate values, then an LLM extracts case number, address, project type, and any stated comment deadline from each agenda item body and its PDF attachments.
  • A matching layer fuzzy-joins permit rows to agenda cases by normalized address + parcel; matched discretionary items become LEVER-feed objects with a hearing date and a jurisdiction-specific inferred comment/appeal-window deadline.
  • The weekly digest leads with an honest count ('Nothing needs you this week' vs 'N items have an open window'); lever items show a Cofacts-style cached plain-language explainer of the jargon and a one-tap routed action (write the body that takes comment, sign up to speak, file an objection).
  • If a resident acts, they can launch a Block Whip — invite neighbors on the same block to act before the deadline — and afterward the item is marked with its disposition (approved/continued/denied) so users see what happened, the honest version of 'see the impact.'

The informal organizer who can rally 50-300 neighbors can rally bodies but can't synthesize them at docket scale, sees no signal that the work landed, and burns out.

Who it's for

Primary: the volunteer neighborhood/issue organizer or small-association staffer who can get 50-300 people mildly engaged on a specific federal rule but has no tooling to make that engagement count at docket scale. Secondary: the funded nonprofit / trade association / coalition that already wants evidence-backed comment synthesis and is willing to host and pay for a public-interest room (this is the explicit DEFER-revisit trigger — the anchor must want dissent/provenance, not raw volume).

The solution

Consensus Comment is a public, auditable deliberation room attached to exactly ONE federal comment period, hosted by a trusted nonprofit/association anchor. It does three things and refuses to do a fourth. (1) It seeds plain-language statements about the docket using an LLM grounded in the agency's own legal text (disclosed as AI-seeded), so a non-expert can react with agree/disagree/pass. (2) It runs open Pol.is-style clustering to surface, with a hard math gate, where participants actually agree across opinion clusters — and it preserves every minority/dissent report rather than burying it. (3) It then helps each participant write and file their OWN individually-attested comment to Regulations.gov by hand, capturing their own receipt — N people, N names, N comments, never one batch packet and never one-click automation. The whole computation — raw votes, cluster math, AI seed prompts, every dissent — is published as a reproducible public method artifact a journalist or clerk can re-run. It consumes its deadline object from DocketClock and never re-derives it. The refusal: no batch packet, no one-click submission, no agency-sold dashboard, no cross-jurisdiction sprawl.

How it works

  • A funded anchor org creates a room and pastes a Regulations.gov docket/document ID; the room binds to that item's DocketClock deadline object (commentEndDate, openForComment, allowLateComments, extension/reopening flags) and renders the operative deadline with source links and an explicit 'file early, not at the wire' nudge.
  • An LLM grounded in the agency's actual notice/legal text generates 15-25 plain-language seed statements; every seed is labeled AI-generated and the generation prompt is logged to the public artifact. The anchor's facilitator can prune/edit before opening.
  • Participants vote agree/disagree/pass on statements and can submit their own statements for others to vote on (standard Pol.is loop). Address-to-district geocoding runs as honest aggregate annotation only ('N self-attested District-X residents'), never as a gate or a 'verified' claim.
  • Open clustering runs continuously. A math gate checks participant count AND cross-cluster diversity: above threshold the room surfaces 'cross-factional consensus' statements plus all minority reports; below threshold it says 'insufficient cross-cluster signal' and degrades to a structured comment-quality workshop.
  • Each participant uses the consensus frame and dissent reports to draft their OWN comment in their own words (the tool assists structure, never authors the substance), then files it by hand on Regulations.gov and pastes back the tracking/receipt number, which the room logs as aggregate 'N attested filings' with timestamps before the deadline.
  • On close, the room publishes the immutable public method artifact: raw vote matrix, cluster computation, AI seed prompts, full dissent set, and the disclosed (unverified, honestly-labeled) participant geography — re-runnable by anyone, the credibility carrier the astroturf attack cannot touch.

The solution

ParcelWatch is a consumer-first change-detection and guided-action app, not a data warehouse. How it works

  • Resident enters a street address; we geocode it to a lat/lng point (Census/Nominatim free tier first, paid geocoder as a fallback) and resolve the governing jurisdiction via a MapIt-style geocode->jurisdiction lookup so we know which Legistar instance to watch.

Lot Line

Type your address once; get plain-language alerts before the bulldozers — the land-use wedge into an always-on neighborhood civic monitor.

The problem

Homeowners and renters typically learn about a rezoning, variance, or 5-story development next door only when bulldozers arrive or when a jargon-filled paper notice card gets tossed with the junk mail. The statutory mailed notice (often a 300-500 ft radius) is the only systematic signal most residents get, and it has no map, no deadline reminder, and no action path. People who care about their block have no proactive, plain-language way to learn "something consequential is being proposed near me" while there's still time to act. Existing tools are either government-buyer per-project silos (PublicInput, coUrbanize) or expensive lobbyist-facing platforms — none serve the non-expert resident at their own address.

Who it's for

Primary: homeowners, renters, and small landlords in mid-to-large US cities who care about their immediate block but are not planning nerds — the person who would have shown up to the hearing if they'd known in time and understood the stakes. Secondary (and the real near-term revenue): high-frequency, high-willingness-to-pay users for whom development monitoring is NOT episodic — neighborhood/HOA associations, land trusts, local advocates, and local journalists — plus B2G/B2-civic buyers (cities, libraries, civic orgs) who want a normalized resident-notification feed.

The solution

Lot Line is positioned not as a standalone "development next door" alert (which dies on episodic demand) but as the LAND-USE PROXIMITY LAYER of a broader, always-on neighborhood civic monitor. A user types their home address once; Lot Line runs a point-in-radius proximity test (using application centroid/point geometry, never requiring boundary polygons) and sends plain-language alerts for rezonings, variances, and major permits filed within a transparent, user-adjustable distance band — explicitly a proximity FILTER, not a legal-radius claim. The same address and the same MAPLE-style follow->event->feed->digest engine also carry comment-period deadlines (federal/state via Regulations.gov + Open States) and local hearings (via Legistar), plus topic alerts — giving a weekly-digest cadence and recurring value between the rare high-stakes development events. Action is differentiated by case type: legislative cases get verified individualized comment plus organize-your-block; quasi-judicial variances get "right person, right hearing, right 3 facts" attendance prompts and testimony aid. Real-affected-neighbor status is verified via address geocode plus SMS/postal tiers (no paid title data) so the input is weighted above the form-letter floods officials discount. Funding is fixed up front: freemium consumer, advocacy/org seats, and a SocietyWorks-style B2G feed/white-label arm that cross-subsidizes the free consumer product.

How it works

  • Onboard: user enters their address; MapIt-style geocoding resolves it to coordinates, jurisdictions, and OCD-IDs, and 'what governs me' is hidden from the user (WriteToThem pattern).
  • Monitor: per-city ArcGIS REST / Socrata adapters pull zoning-case and major-permit records on a polling cadence; a point-in-radius spatial test against the user's adjustable band flags nearby items. Federal comment periods (Regulations.gov/Federal Register), state bills (Open States), and local hearings (Legistar) feed the same event stream as OCD Events.
  • Translate: a RuleBox-style LLM enrichment service (run separately from the core app) classifies each case type and generates a plain-language summary, the stakes, the deadline, and the single action that matters.
  • Alert + digest: the follow->event->feed->digest engine sends an urgent push for a nearby development event and a weekly digest of deadlines, hearings, and topic matches so the subscription earns its keep between rare events.
  • Verify + act: address-geocode confirms affected-neighbor status; SMS (and optional postal) tiers raise assurance. Case-type-appropriate flow fires: drafted individualized comment + organize-your-block for legislative cases; hearing attendance reminder + 3-fact testimony aid for quasi-judicial variances.
  • See impact: the app tracks the case to its vote/decision and reports back what happened and how many verified neighbors weighed in — closing the monitor->act->see-impact loop.

RuleRadar for Small Operators

The problem

Owner-operators and very small businesses are directly regulated by FDA, USDA, DOT/FMCSA, OSHA, EPA, and state boards, but they never see a proposed rule until it's final and compliance is mandatory. Regulations.gov holds the data but offers zero discovery: there is no way to say "I run a 3-truck reefer outfit in Ohio" and get back the open dockets that change your obligations. Trade associations only cover dues-paying members, leaving the long tail of non-members flying blind while only well-staffed incumbents file comments. The result is that the people most affected by a rule are structurally absent from the process that shapes it.

Who it's for

Primary: owner-operators and very small business owners in heavily-regulated verticals who are NOT members of a well-staffed trade association — the independent restaurant owner, the 3-truck logistics outfit, the home childcare provider, the small specialty-crop farmer. Secondary (and the actual buyer): the aggregators who already hold the operator relationship — community/SBA lenders, specialty insurers and factoring companies, franchisors, trade associations seeking to serve their non-dues-paying long tail, and the accountants/bookkeepers operators already trust on compliance.

The solution

RuleRadar is a vertical regulatory-discovery-and-legibility service, not a consumer comment-submission app. For a defined class of small operator, it continuously scans the open-docket firehose and surfaces only the proposed federal and state rules that actually change that operator's obligations — explaining what changes, why, the exact triggering clause, and the comment deadline as a first-class alertable object. A two-stage funnel (cheap high-recall shortlist, then expensive long-context applicability reasoning) emits calibrated confidence and the specific clause, so the user never sees a bare "this affects you." When the operator wants to act, RuleRadar drafts a substantive comment but refuses to finish it until the operator supplies their own quantitative facts (dollars, hours, truck/table/child counts, named local impact), then routes it through whatever channel that agency accepts — pre-addressed docket email, print-to-sign-and-mail PDF, or a deep link to the exact web form with the draft on the clipboard. It then closes the loop using GET-only data: fingerprint-matching the posted comment in the docket to confirm it landed, and, when the final rule publishes, LLM-extracting whether the agency's response-to-comments section addressed the operator's specific issue. Distribution is B2B2C: lenders, insurers, franchisors, and associations white-label it to reach operators the cold consumer funnel never could.

How it works

  • Onboard via a partner: a lender, insurer, factoring company, or association passes RuleRadar a structured operator profile (vertical, state, size attributes: trucks/tables/seats/children/acreage/license types) it already holds — no cold-acquisition form for the operator to fill out.
  • Match: a cheap RuleBox/embedding pass over the Mirrulations / spicy-regs open-docket corpus shortlists candidate dockets for the vertical; a long-context LLM then reasons over only those candidates for applicability (thresholds, exemptions, effective dates, preemption) and emits a calibrated confidence score plus the exact triggering clause.
  • Alert + explain: the operator gets a plain-language brief — what changes, why it matters to an operation like theirs, the quoted clause, and the comment deadline tracked as a first-class object with reminders before it closes.
  • Guided comment: if the operator wants to act, the drafting flow collects their own quantitative facts (cost in $/hours, counts, named local impact) and refuses to generate boilerplate; optionally it aggregates distinct operators' facts into one rostered coalition comment rather than N near-duplicates.
  • Submit via the channel that agency actually accepts: pre-addressed docket email from the NPRM ADDRESSES section, a print-to-sign-and-mail PDF, or a deep link to the exact web form with the draft copied to clipboard.
  • Close the loop: fingerprint-match the user's comment in the posted docket to confirm receipt; when the final rule publishes, extract from the agency's response-to-comments section whether the operator's specific issue was addressed, and report that back.

The problem

The solution

Neighbor Bloc is two things bolted together. The problem

Nobody turns the hyper-local council firehose into something a small org can act on.

Who it's for

Primary: the executive director / advocacy staffer of a local or metro-level business association, chamber of commerce, BID, or restaurant association chapter — the person who already owes members "we watch City Hall for you" and has no scalable way to deliver it. Secondary: the member owner-operator (independent restaurant, cafe, food truck, corner store, salon, retail), who receives the product free as a membership benefit and is the source of the co-sign action graph.

The solution

CityWatch is a white-label radar-and-action toolkit licensed annually to business associations, chambers, and BIDs, who give it to members free as a benefit. The org uploads its member roster; each business is resolved by address and business type to its governing jurisdictions and council district via a MapIt-style geocoder. The system pulls Legistar OData for council, committee, and board agendas/items, then uses an LLM rule-generation layer (RuleBox pattern) to classify and normalize raw local vocabulary ("parklet," "mobile food vending," "A-frame sign," "conditional use") into a canonical, consumer-readable taxonomy. The genuine net-new primitive is the alertable deadline object: every comment window and hearing date becomes a first-class, dated, plain-language card routed only to the members it actually affects. On the act side, a one-click member co-sign aggregates into a verified, district-attributed brief the org's staffer or lobbyist carries into the room — quality-over-volume evidence officials can't dismiss as a form-letter flood. How it works

  • Org signs an annual contract and uploads its member roster (CSV of business names, addresses, types); CityWatch geocodes each address to council district + overlapping jurisdictions/boards using a MapIt-clone, building an instant network with zero cold-start.
  • A polling service hits Legistar OData on a schedule for new/changed agenda items across the org's covered bodies; a City Scrapers-style diff also catches cancelled/rescheduled meetings.
  • An LLM classifier (RuleBox-generated rules running on cheap infra) tags each item against the canonical Main-Street taxonomy and extracts any comment-period or hearing deadline into a structured deadline object.
  • Each relevant item becomes a plain-language card ('The council is voting June 24 on doubling sidewalk-cafe permit fees — comment closes June 17') routed only to members whose address/type matches, plus a weekly digest of everything the council did.
  • A member taps to co-sign or add a one-line comment; the system verifies them as a real district business (roster + address geocode) and aggregates co-signs into a district-attributed brief the org's rep submits or carries into the hearing.
  • After the vote, the outcome and the org's contribution are recorded in a versioned public archive and surfaced back to members and the org board as proof of impact.
