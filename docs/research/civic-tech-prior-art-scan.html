Civic Tech Research Team · Prior-Art Scan

Track + Act on Bureaucratic Processes

Who is already building a tool that helps ordinary, non-expert people track public comment periods, hearings & rulemaking — and take meaningful action?

Prepared 2026-06-06 Sources: SF Civic Tech · Hack for LA · ACT / brigade network · commercial & gov market

Bottom line

Nobody — in the volunteer civic-tech world or the commercial market — is doing the exact thing you described: a consumer-facing tool that does both halves (monitor + act) across regulation and local land-use, with proactive discovery for non-experts.

The market splits cleanly along seams, and the seam you're aiming at is genuinely open. The catch: several volunteer teams have started pieces of this and let them die — which tells you the hard part is sustaining it, not conceiving it.

01What the brigades have built

The volunteer civic-tech landscape — including the two groups you named.

The brigade network you're picturing no longer exists as such. Code for America dissolved its ~60-chapter Brigade Network in 2023 (pivoting to direct government software delivery after a $100M gift). Volunteers regrouped under the Alliance of Civic Technologists (ACT), a decentralized federation of ~18 groups. The strongest hubs today are ones that never depended on CfA: Chi Hack Night, BetaNYC, SF Civic Tech, Open Austin. The Civic Tech Field Guide remains the central project directory. This is your recruiting / partner channel if you go the volunteer route.

SF Civic Tech org active 2026

Active in 2026, but nothing in their portfolio matches. Closest neighbors are WeVote (ballots — wrong domain) and PolicyEngine (policy impact simulation — no tracking/action loop). Essentially greenfield there. Note: SF volunteers have repeatedly started gov-engagement tools and let them go dormant — a caution about sustainability.

Hack for LA — "Engage" on hold / frozen 2022

HfLA is active in 2026, and they have the single closest concept match. Engage scrapes council agendas, surfaces them to constituents, and lets them comment. But it's officially "On Hold," core repos frozen since Dec 2022, no live site. Scoped only to LA Neighborhood Council agendas. Its engage-scraper ("government agnostic") is worth a look as a reference artifact for the ingestion problem.

04Architecture & build strategy

Deep teardowns of the six most relevant codebases — stack, the patterns worth borrowing, what to avoid, and a reuse verdict for each. Findings are from reading the actual repos (source, schemas, docs) as of 2026-06-06.

Verdict key:  build on it  hybrid  reference only

MAPLE — the closest end-to-end blueprint

build on it

Stack: Next.js (static export on Vercel) + Firebase for all backend — Firestore as system-of-record, Cloud Functions in two codebases (Node/TS for logic, a separate Python codebase just for LLM bill-summarization), Firebase Auth, Storage, and a firestore-send-email extension. Self-hosted Typesense for search (incl. vector/semantic). Mature and in-production; backend is well-architected, frontend is cruft-y (three styling systems coexist).

Borrow

  • Search-indexer factory — each domain registers schema + convert() + a Firestore onWrite trigger that incrementally syncs to Typesense. Swap "bills" for "rules/hearings/ordinances."
  • Notification engine — follow → topic → central event collection → per-user feed → batched daily/weekly/monthly digest emails → TTL cleanup. This is your monitoring loop.
  • Cursor-based batch scraper engineered around serverless timeout/concurrency to keep a big dataset fresh on a rolling basis.
  • AI enrichment as a separate service (their Python functions codebase).

Avoid

  • The mailto: delivery — MAPLE doesn't actually submit testimony; it opens the user's mail client. A dodge because MA has no API. You should deliver server-side via real APIs and capture a receipt.
  • Near-zero constituent verification — district/rep is self-reported; the email just asserts "as your constituent." You'll need address→district geocoding it lacks.
  • The mixed frontend (Bootstrap + MUI + styled-components, Pages Router, old Firebase SDK).

Verdict: The serverless + Firestore + Typesense + event-driven-notifications + separate-AI-codebase shape is a sensible blueprint for the whole product. Mine the backend; rebuild action-delivery and identity.

Mirrulations — the federal rulemaking backbone

hybrid

What it is: an open mirror of all of Regulations.gov (~253k dockets / ~1.8M docs / ~22M comments) because the API is brutally rate-limited (1,000 req/hr/key, hard 5,000-item paging, no bulk export). Architecture: RabbitMQ job queue + Redis checkpoints + donated-API-key pool; a work-generator enqueues jobs every 6h, distributed clients self-pace. Output is a free public S3 bucket (AWS Open Data, unsigned reads, hourly refresh) of raw JSON.

Borrow

  • The S3 mirror itself — terabytes of history, free to read. Use for the bulk/historical corpus.
  • mirrulations-search's Postgres schema — has is_open_for_comment, comment_start/end_date, user "collections" (watchlists), + Federal Register & CFR joins. A near-ready monitoring data model.
  • spicy-regs (actively maintained) — Parquet-on-object-store + DuckDB + an MCP server so an LLM can query the corpus via SQL.

Avoid

  • Don't inherit the crawler for live alerting — 6h cycle + hourly publish + no retry loop is too laggy/fragile for "comment period just opened."
  • Don't assume the mirror is queryable — it's loose JSON in S3; you build the index/ETL.

Verdict — hybrid: build on the mirror for history; hit the Regulations.gov v4 + Federal Register APIs directly for the thin freshness layer that drives alerts. Gaps it leaves: maintained query API, alerting, plain-language summaries, spam detection, state/local + non-Regulations.gov agencies (FCC/SEC — explicitly unsolved), and any "take action."

Open States / Plural — the legislation data backbone

build on it

What it is: the canonical open dataset for 50 states + DC + 4 territories + Congress, built on the Open Civic Data (OCD) standard (Popolo-derived: Division / Jurisdiction / Organization / Person / Bill / VoteEvent / Event, all with stable ocd-* IDs). ~200 scrapers normalize wildly different sites by emitting schema-validated JSON. Actively maintained; open data is public-domain and free. Plural is the commercial arm that subsidizes it.

Borrow

  • The OCD data model itself — adopt its object types + OCD-IDs as your internal canonical schema for free interop. The single most valuable reusable asset here.
  • Consume the v3 REST API — updated_since polling for incremental monitoring; people.geo for "who represents me."
  • Daily Postgres bulk dump as backfill / fallback.

Avoid

  • Don't self-host the ~200 scrapers — that long tail of constant breakage is the biggest hidden cost in this ecosystem.
  • Don't rely on the free API tier (10/min, 500/day) for production — budget a paid tier.

Verdict: The right, free backbone for the legislation + hearings half. But no regulations/rulemaking, no comment periods, no real municipal coverage — those are out of scope (rulemaking is Plural's paid product) and must come from elsewhere.

City Scrapers + Councilmatic — and the Legistar unlock

hybrid

The headline finding reframes the local problem. Legistar (Granicus) is the key unlock: agenda/legislative SaaS used by ~70% of large US cities/counties, and every client exposes a public, auth-free OData Web API (webapi.legistar.com/v1/{client}) returning clean JSON — bills, actions, sponsors, votes, agendas, committees, people — queryable incrementally by modified-date.

City Scrapers (Scrapy, GitHub-Actions cron, outputs OCD-Event JSON to object storage) is the proven pattern for the bespoke long tail — one spider per agency + a diff pipeline that marks vanished meetings cancelled. But it's effectively Chicago-only now. Councilmatic (Django on old pins, Solr/ES) is a council-watching read UI over a pupa-populated OCD Postgres DB; notifications are RSS-over-saved-search.

Borrow

  • Build directly on the Legistar Web API — covers the large-city majority. Use python-legistar-scraper as reference for OData filters/paging; you don't need pupa/OCD-Django/Councilmatic to call it.
  • City Scrapers' diff/dedup + status-monitoring pattern (Sentry, "mark vanished as cancelled") for the bespoke spiders you can't avoid.
  • Councilmatic's faceted-search field design + PDF/RTF text extraction.

Avoid

  • Don't fork Councilmatic for a consumer product — old Django/Solr pins, single-jurisdiction, expert-facing, RSS-only alerts.
  • Don't expect the generic kits to be turnkey — thin/aging maintainer base.

Verdict — hybrid: Legistar API as backbone for the ~70%; City-Scrapers-style spiders for the long tail (PrimeGov, CivicClerk, Municode, PDF-only — each days-to-weeks plus permanent maintenance). Neither gives you comment-period deadlines, land-use/zoning/permit data (Accela/Socrata/ArcGIS — separate problem), cross-city normalization, or any consumer UX.

Decidim & Consul — the action / organizing layer

reference only

Both are heavy Rails participatory-democracy platforms, both AGPL-3.0 — and that license is the headline. For a proprietary consumer SaaS, AGPL's network-copyleft means hosting a derivative obligates you to release your source. Borrowing concepts is free; shipping their code isn't.

Borrow (concepts)

  • Consul's Collaborative Legislation — government text → clause-by-clause citizen annotation → phased debate→draft→follow-up. The tightest 1:1 match to a comment-period lifecycle that exists anywhere.
  • Decidim's pluggable verification (SMS / ID-doc / postal-letter / census, with scoped permissions like "only verified residents of district X may act") — the best real-person-assurance design in the field.
  • Decidim's Accountability component — track what happened to a proposal. Directly serves your "effort wasn't wasted" requirement.
  • Consul's escalating verification tiers (browse → SMS to comment → census+letter for binding votes).

Avoid

  • Don't adopt/fork either — AGPL, not headless/embeddable, assume an authoritative resident census, and are operationally enterprise-grade.
  • They're destinations a government stands up, not a component fed by your monitoring data.

Verdict — reference architectures, not dependencies: build thin, AGPL-free, purpose-built action flows (à la MAPLE/Resistbot) fed by your own data. The one thing not worth reinventing: port the verification-tier design early.

What nobody has built — your actual whitespace

Every agent independently flagged the same five gaps. The data backbones for the legislation half are mature and free; these are where you'd actually be building:

  1. Public comment-period deadlines as a first-class, alertable object — not modeled in Open States, Legistar, OCD, or City Scrapers.
  2. Land use / zoning / permits — entirely uncovered; separate, messy ingestion.
  3. Cross-source, cross-jurisdiction normalization for non-experts — everyone keeps raw local vocabulary.
  4. Plain-language legibility + guided action — the consumer-facing layer.
  5. The monitor → act → see-impact loop in one consumer product.

This lines up exactly with the market gaps from the commercial scan below.

What to steal from mySociety (UK) & g0v (Taiwan)

🇬🇧 mySociety — proves your exact product surface

  • TheyWorkForYou (monitor) + WriteToThem (action: postcode → your reps → send) + MapIt (the postcode→jurisdiction engine underneath). WriteToThem's abstraction — the user never has to know who represents them — is the cleanest version of your hardest UX problem.
  • Vector-search topic alerts (rebuilt Oct 2025): subscribe to a concept, get related-term expansion + weekly digest. The exact monitoring UX, freshly validated.
  • RuleBox — LLM writes classification rules that then run on cheap infra. The cost model for a text firehose.
  • Funding model: commercial arm (SocietyWorks) sells the FixMyStreet stack to councils, subsidizing free public products. A B2G SaaS funding a free consumer tool.
  • Caution (Keep It In The Community): they wanted a live national land-use register, found council data too messy, shipped a snapshot. Expect that.

🇹🇼 g0v — the deliberation layer

  • vTaiwan + Pol.is is the headline steal. Pol.is clusters opinion and surfaces cross-factional consensus statements — which carry far more weight with officials than a pile of identical form letters, and scale to non-experts without becoming a flame war. Fed ~26 pieces of legislation. (Caveat: vTaiwan is now unfunded/volunteer — deliberation tools need a durable funding home.)
  • Join.gov.tw — a 5,000-signature → mandatory ministry response threshold. A legible, hard mechanic that converts diffuse concern into accountable action.
  • Cofacts — meet users inside the messaging app they already use + a community-verified answer cache that gets cheaper per query over time. Applies to "explain this proposed rule."

The synthesis across both

mySociety proves the monitor-and-contact surface and a funding model; g0v / Pol.is provides the deliberate-to-consensus action layer that turns "email your rep" into "your community reaches a position officials must reckon with." The differentiated product is the combination — anchored on vector-search topic alerts (monitor) and Pol.is-style consensus deliberation (act).

06The gaps — where your idea lives

Where the research points to genuine white space.

1

Discovery for non-experts

The biggest gap. Everything assumes you already know a docket/bill exists. Nobody proactively says "a rule/zoning change/hearing that affects you is open for comment — here's the deadline." AI summarization makes this newly tractable.

2

Both halves, for individuals

Monitoring = B2B/pricey; action = consumer/free but Congress-only & discovery-free; gov platforms = both, but one city's one project. No affordable consumer product does monitor + act together across regulation and local land-use.

3

State & especially LOCAL

Federal is clunky-but-covered. Local zoning / land-use / public-health processes are almost entirely uncovered for residents — exactly the domains you named.

4

Agency rulemaking & public health

Rulemaking (vs. legislation) is barely served on the consumer side at all. The action tools are legislator-centric, not docket/rulemaking-centric.

Sharpest wedge

A resident-facing, location-based "watch + act" tool for local/state land-use and rulemaking, where the discovery + plain-language layer (the thing nobody does for non-experts) is the moat, and the action layer mirrors what Resistbot / 5 Calls proved works — but pointed at comment dockets and hearings instead of just Congress.

Main competitive risk isn't head-on (no one owns this box) — it's adjacency creep: Granicus could ship a resident app, FiscalNote could de-tier PolicyNote's AI, or Resistbot could extend to dockets.
