# Product / Market ballot

Product context: these are mostly external or hybrid civic/regulatory products sold through professional channels (associations, newsrooms, legal aid, foundations, clerks, PSAOs). Adoption is not captive, so wedge clarity, trusted distribution, and perceived reliability matter as much as raw civic value.

## Rationale by survivor

**1. Pharmacy Docket Desk — strongest commercial wedge.** The demand is credible because independent pharmacies face recurring, money-linked regulatory change across CMS, DEA, FDA, Medicaid, boards, and PBM policy. The wedge is clear if it is a member-specific action file rather than another policy newsletter. Frequency is high enough to sustain attention, and distribution through PSAOs, buying groups, wholesalers, or state associations is unusually plausible for this slate. Required changes: do not sell direct one-by-one first; validate a channel partner, keep expert review, and frame outputs as source-cited operational action files rather than legal advice.

**2. Childcare Rule Radar / CareNotice Counsel — strong channel-led operator product.** Demand is high because licensing, subsidy, ratio, background-check, training, and reimbursement changes can affect survival for small providers. The wedge is sharp when narrowed to one state and one provider type, and distribution through associations, CCR&Rs, or shared-services alliances can overcome exhausted-buyer friction. Differentiation is moderate-good: not generic rule tracking, but operational impact translation. Required changes: association-led MVP only, human-reviewed impact briefs, and proof that alerts reduce license/cost risk rather than adding another inbox burden.

**3. CommentWindow Registry / DocketClock — best infrastructure primitive.** The problem is foundational: missed or wrong participation deadlines destroy trust for downstream civic, media, and association workflows. Wedge clarity is excellent as a federal-only deadline-object API with provenance, confidence, webhooks, and change logs. Demand is professional rather than mass-market, but recurring and infrastructure-like. Distribution is decent through civic-data teams, vertical rule products, and newsrooms, though not as clean as trade-association verticals. Required changes: stay federal-only for MVP, make uncertainty/staleness first-class, and avoid broad SLA claims until human review/correction workflows are proven.

**4. CareRule Scout / Benefits Plainwatch — high-stakes but must be intermediary-first.** Demand is real because Medicaid/HCBS and benefits changes can materially affect service hours, eligibility, reimbursement, and recertification. The wedge is less crisp than pharmacy/childcare because beneficiaries, families, providers, advocates, and navigators have different needs, and rule frequency may be bursty. Distribution is plausible through P&A orgs, legal aid, disability/aging nonprofits, and foundations. Required changes: choose one program/region, sell to expert intermediaries, avoid direct-to-recipient mass alerts, and keep calm legal-boundary framing to prevent panic or false reassurance.

**5. RecordReady Clerk Intake — real workflow pain, harder go-to-market.** The 72-hour pre-meeting packet crunch is concrete, recurring, and staff-owned, making demand and frequency strong. The wedge is clear: ingest messy inbound comments and produce staff-reviewed record-ready packets inside existing clerk workflows. Differentiation is good because it starts from government inbox reality rather than a new public engagement portal. Distribution is the weakness: B2G procurement, trust, official-record risk, and change management will slow adoption. Required changes: one Legistar jurisdiction, one mailbox, one meeting body, immutable audit trail, and no autonomous record authority.

**6. Tenant Displacement Docket — urgent pain, constrained market path.** Demand is emotionally and practically strong: missed permit, demolition, conversion, or appeal windows can mean irreversible displacement. The wedge is meaningful if anchored to one city/case type and legal-aid or tenant-union workflows. Frequency is moderate because actionable cases vary by city and source data may arrive too late. Distribution is partner-dependent and grant/nonprofit-heavy, which limits scalability. Required changes: strict access controls, no public vulnerable-building intelligence, organization-mediated outreach, human-reviewed alerts, and proof that alerts are early enough to change outcomes.

**7. Concern Router / Civic RouteMap — useful bridge, but premise risk remains.** Demand exists among navigators, reporters, hotlines, and help desks because people often do not know which official lever matters. But the wedge is broad and accuracy-sensitive: routing a plain-language concern to dockets, council items, state bills, school boards, or agencies can easily become overpromising. Frequency is good, differentiation is real, and distribution is plausible through civic-information funders or professional navigators, but trust loss from bad routing is the core market risk. Required changes: navigator-facing only, one metro plus bounded sources, conservative confidence, citations, and “no reliable path found” as a normal answer.

**8. Conditions Keeper / Outcome Graph — valuable add-on, weakest standalone.** The after-action loop is neglected, and reminders for continuances, appeal deadlines, votes, and condition text changes are useful. However, demand is softer than pre-deadline/action products because the user has already passed the highest-urgency moment, and distribution is unclear outside watchdogs, clinics, local journalism, or as an API module. Differentiation is moderate, but the standalone business case is thin. Required changes: position as infrastructure/add-on, start with one planning commission, track only source-linked receipts and next windows, and avoid enforcement, noncompliance, or “your comment mattered” claims.
